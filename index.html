Who am I?

My name is Johnnie Zaharia, and I’m currently a Junior studying software engineering at NDSU. My goal with this degree is to pursue a career in application software development while applying the practical knowledge gained in my studies and work experiences. I’m passionate about learning how to use technology to improve the lives of others and solve widely-faced problems.

About Me

Personal Background

I’m from a small town in southern Minnesota called Truman, which took up only one square mile of land and was home to about a thousand people, meaning I graduated with a grade size of only eight people. I have three sisters including my twin Gracie and two older sisters named Emily and Courtney. Growing up, I was always curious and was interested in learning more about nearly everything. I took a strong liking to math and different subjects that allowed me to analyze situations and problem-solve, diving deeper into those concepts by participating in different tech and STEM-related camps. After learning about what coding was and its basics, I felt I needed to know more. Around the age of 12, after self-learning HTML and CSS using resources on the internet I created my first sample website, which was a simple bakery website with an ordering form.

Professional Background

Throughout high school and the beginning of college, I worked a variety of different food-service jobs. In multiple of these positions, including at Subway and Insomnia Cookies, my role was a Shift Lead. This meant that my job not only included the basic aspects of being an employee like serving the customers and completing orders, but also more important activities like handling money, tracking inventory, and maintaining customer satisfaction. I started my current position as a Research Assistant with NDSU’s ECE department and the USDA in May of 2025. In this role, I work on agricultural sensor research and development. This means that I get to work with real clients to assess their needs and gather requirements, then work alongside my team to engineer and deliver effective and innovative solutions to the problems they face. I am planning to take another step toward my career goals with a summer internship at AGCO, where I will work in Software Requirements Testing.

Portfolio & Projects

Mock Space Invaders Game

Created as a final project for my Introduction to Computer Science course, this is an interactive model of the popular space invaders game which was programmed using Java.

Furever Friends Adoption Website

Developed with a team as a final project for my Modern Software Development course, this is a functional dog adoption website. This project required our team to utilize important software engineering concepts like agile methodology and database design and was developed using HTML, CSS, and Bootstrap while using SQL to query the related databases.

Bean Field Temperature Sensor Network

This was my first project as a Research Assistant, where my team was tasked with developing a way to monitor widespread temperature changes in agricultural fields. Our solution was a large sensor network, consisting of many flexible poles each holding three sensors to monitor temperatures at different heights. These poles are then arranged in a grid pattern throughout the field and transmit their data wirelessly via Bluetooth to our devices for monitoring.

Remote Activated Electromagnet

For this work project, I was tasked with developing a remote-activated electromagnet that is capable of picking up, moving, and setting down an 8-pound radar unit. The radar will be used along sugar beet piles to scan and locate areas of rotting, which not only causes more rotting but also releases harmful chemicals into the air. I developed a schematic and tested it using a breadboard, then designed and assembled a printed circuit board (PCB), and finally made any necessary changes and further refined the product.

3D-Printed Electromagnet Enclosure

This was the final part of my electromagnet project, and it consisted of developing and 3D-printing an enclosure to hold the electromagnet PCB, battery pack, and electromagnet unit together in a compact but functional manner. This enclosure has a ridge for a battery pack on the bottom, a panel for mounting the PCB securely inside, and a sliding plexiglass side. It also has a mounting hole on the bottom for the electromagnet unit, an external power button, and external LEDs that indicate the board’s activity. This unit will be mounted to the bottom of a drone so that the user can remotely move objects like the radar unit.
